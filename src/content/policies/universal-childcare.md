## Our Solution

### The Denver Universal Childcare Act

Modeled on the systems in France, Sweden, Denmark, and Finland â€” adapted for Denver.

**1. Universal Access from 6 Months**
- Guaranteed childcare slot for every Denver child from 6 months to kindergarten entry
- No waitlists â€” capacity is the city's responsibility
- Full-day programs (7 AM - 6 PM) to match working parents' schedules
- Located in public facilities, schools, community centers, and licensed neighborhood providers

**2. Income-Based Fees (Capped)**
- Free for families earning under 200% FPL (~$62,000 for family of 4)
- Sliding scale above that, capped at $200/month per child regardless of income
- No family pays more than 5% of household income on childcare
- Compare: Sweden caps childcare at ~$150/month. France's *Ã‰cole maternelle* is free from age 3. Denmark caps at ~$500/month (with subsidies reducing most families' cost to near-zero).

**3. Living Wages for Childcare Workers**
- Starting salary: $25/hr (matching Denver's minimum wage target)
- Parity with K-12 teachers for credentialed early childhood educators
- Full benefits: health insurance, retirement, paid leave
- Professional development and career ladder
- Rationale: You cannot have quality childcare with poverty-wage workers. Period.

**4. Quality Standards**
- Maximum ratios: 3:1 for infants, 4:1 for toddlers, 8:1 for preschool
- Curriculum: play-based, developmentally appropriate, trauma-informed
- Bilingual programming (English/Spanish) in all centers
- Outdoor play requirements
- Inclusion mandate: all centers serve children with disabilities, with appropriate support

Quebec's $7/day universal childcare program, launched in 1997, is the most studied example in North America:

- **Tax revenue increase** from those mothers' earnings exceeded the program's cost

Nobel laureate economist **James Heckman's** research shows that every $1 invested in early childhood education returns **$7-13 in long-term economic benefits** through higher earnings, lower crime, better health outcomes, and reduced social service costs.

**This is not an expense. It is the highest-return public investment available.**

## How We Pay For It

**Estimated annual cost:** $150-200M/year for universal coverage

**Funding sources:**
1. **City allocation:** $80-100M from general fund (Denver's budget is $1.6B+)
2. **Federal/state matching:** Head Start, CCDBG, and Colorado early childhood funds ($40-50M)
3. **Employer payroll assessment:** 0.3% payroll tax dedicated to childcare fund ($50-60M)
4. **Revenue offset:** Increased tax revenue from parents entering/staying in workforce (Quebec's program shows this eventually covers the full cost)

**Net long-term cost: Zero or negative.** When parents can work, they pay taxes. When children receive quality early education, they require fewer interventions later. The investment pays for itself within 5-10 years.

**"Parents should raise their own children."**

**"Why should taxpayers fund other people's childcare?"**

**"Won't this put private childcare centers out of business?"**
No. Licensed private providers can participate in the system and receive public funding for serving families. This is how France, Sweden, and Denmark operate â€” a mix of public and licensed private providers, all meeting quality standards, all affordable for families.
